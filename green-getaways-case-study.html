The problem

Manual bookings were costing more than the bookings were worth

Green Getaways ran a collection of eco-friendly stays across South India - forest cabins, hillside camps, backwater cottages. A good product. But the operational layer holding it together was a patchwork of WhatsApp threads, shared Google Sheets, and email chains.

Every booking followed the same exhausting path: a guest enquired via the website contact form or WhatsApp, a staff member manually checked availability on a shared spreadsheet, sent a confirmation message, updated the spreadsheet, and then chased the guest for payment. If two staff members handled overlapping enquiries without seeing each other's updates, you got a double-booking. This happened regularly.

Beyond the errors, the workload was unsustainable. The team was spending 3-4 hours a day just processing booking enquiries - time that should have been going into guest experience. They needed a system that handled the routine so the team didn't have to.

Discovery

Mapping the mess before building the fix

Before writing a line of code or opening Figma, I spent time with the team tracing what actually happened during a booking - start to finish. I mapped every touchpoint: initial enquiry, availability check, confirmation, payment, reminder, and the post-stay follow-up. The map was uncomfortable to look at. There were 11 manual handoffs across two spreadsheets, three messaging apps, and an email inbox.

Two problems dominated. First: no single source of truth for availability. When the spreadsheet was behind by even an hour, staff were confirming bookings that couldn't be honoured. Second: no automation for any part of the process - every confirmation, reminder, and receipt was written and sent manually. The team wasn't being careless; the system was just built for a much smaller operation.

I also looked at what guests were experiencing. The contact form on the existing site said "we'll get back to you within 24 hours." In the booking industry, 24 hours is an eternity. Guests were going to competitors who offered instant confirmation simply because the waiting was too uncertain.

Approach

WordPress as the platform, automation as the goal

The client already had a WordPress site. The brief was clear: work within WordPress, build something the non-technical team could manage themselves, and make it actually solve the problem rather than add another thing to maintain.

I decided to build a custom booking plugin rather than forcing a third-party plugin to fit the client's specific requirements. The available plugins were either too generic (assuming hotel-style room blocks) or too complex for a small team to manage. A custom plugin meant I could design the admin experience to match exactly how the team worked, not how a plugin developer assumed they worked.

The core architecture: a property taxonomy in WordPress (each property as a custom post type with availability managed per-property), a booking form that queried real-time availability before confirming, and a WooCommerce integration to handle payments inline. Confirmations, reminders, and receipts were automated via email triggers on booking status changes.

Design & build process

Designing the booking flow end to end

01

Booking flow UX - the guest experience

Designed the end-to-end guest journey: property browse, availability check, booking form, payment, and confirmation. The key decision was showing real-time availability as the guest selected dates - not after they'd filled out the form. This reduced the "I went through the whole process and it's not available" drop-off that was frustrating guests on the old contact-form approach.

02

Custom WordPress booking plugin

Built a custom plugin with per-property availability calendars, booking state management (pending, confirmed, cancelled), and an availability query system that checked for conflicts before allowing a booking to proceed. This was the core piece that eliminated double-bookings - the availability state was managed server-side, not in a shared spreadsheet that could be out of sync.

03

Admin dashboard design

Designed the admin view around the team's actual workflow: a monthly calendar showing all bookings across properties, colour-coded by status. The team could see at a glance what was booked, what was pending, what needed attention. Availability blocking for maintenance or personal stays was a single click. No spreadsheets, no manual cross-referencing.

04

Automated confirmation and reminder emails

Set up a full email automation sequence triggered by booking status changes: instant confirmation on booking, payment receipt, 7-day reminder with directions and check-in instructions, and a post-stay follow-up requesting a review. The templates were designed to feel personal, not automated. The team had previously written each of these manually - this eliminated all of it.

The booking flow

From enquiry to confirmation - fully automated

The old process involved 11 manual steps across multiple tools. The new flow reduced this to zero manual steps for standard bookings - the system handled everything from availability check to payment receipt.

Key decisions

What made the difference

The most impactful design decision was making the availability check happen as the guest picked dates, not after the booking form was submitted. The old contact-form flow let guests go through the entire process before finding out a property was unavailable. The new system surfaced availability state in real time, which reduced false-start drop-off and made the confirmation feel trustworthy.

Building a custom plugin rather than configuring an off-the-shelf solution was a harder decision to justify upfront - it took more time to build. But every off-the-shelf booking plugin I evaluated was designed around hotel room logic: fixed rooms, fixed capacity, same availability across all rooms. Green Getaways' properties had variable sleeping capacities, different rate structures, and specific blocked periods for maintenance. A custom plugin meant the data model could reflect reality, not force reality to fit the plugin.

The admin experience was designed with the assumption that whoever was managing bookings would not be technical. Every action - confirming a booking, blocking dates, viewing guest details - was accessible from the calendar view with no more than two clicks. The team reported they needed less than 15 minutes of training to use it confidently.

Outcome

What changed after launch

The immediate, measurable change was the elimination of double-bookings. In the six months before launch, the team had handled at least three double-booking incidents that required compensation, re-booking guests into alternative properties, and the associated guest relations fallout. After launch: zero.

The operational impact was significant. The 3-4 hours of daily booking admin - checking the spreadsheet, confirming by WhatsApp, sending emails manually - went to near-zero. The team's workload shifted away from processing and toward hospitality: improving the properties, responding to guest questions, and thinking about the product rather than managing the back-office.

Conversion also improved. Guests who previously waited up to 24 hours for a confirmation were now getting instant booking confirmations. The "book now and get immediate confirmation" framing tested better with returning guests who had previously found the waiting period frustrating enough to book elsewhere.

Reflection

What I'd do differently

The plugin worked well, but the WooCommerce integration added complexity I'd underestimated - particularly around partial payments, cancellation handling, and refund flows. With hindsight I'd have invested more time in defining the edge cases (what happens to availability when a booking is cancelled? when a payment fails mid-flow?) before building, rather than handling them as they emerged.

I'd also push harder for a guest account feature earlier. Returning guests had to re-enter their details every booking, and the team couldn't build a guest history view. A lightweight guest account system would have enabled both a better repeat-booking experience and useful occupancy analytics for the team.
